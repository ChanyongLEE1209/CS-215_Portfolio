Does Weather Change How Much We Move?

A small data science story about temperature, clouds, rain, and daily steps.

Start the Story ↓
🌈

My Big Question

I wanted to know whether weather affects daily activity. Since productivity is hard to measure directly, I used average daily steps as a simple productivity-related behavior.

I combined a wearable activity dataset from Norway with Open-Meteo weather data. Then I compared daily steps with temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover.

👟 Activity Data

Daily steps and activity records from 113 participants.

🌦️ Weather Data

Temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover from Open-Meteo.

🧪 Method

I grouped people into low, medium, and high activity groups.

❓

Research Questions

  • Does warmer weather lead to more steps?
  • Do low-, medium-, and high-activity people react differently?
  • Do rainy days reduce activity?
  • Do cloudy days change step counts?
📊

1. Steps by Temperature Range

This graph shows average daily steps across different temperature ranges. The main pattern is simple: people usually walked more when the weather was warmer.

🌡️

2. Daily Temperature vs. Steps

This scatter plot shows daily observations. Each point represents one activity group on one day. The trend suggests that warmer days are generally linked with higher step counts.

🔍

3. Regression Prediction

I also used a regression model with interaction terms. This helped me compare whether the temperature-step relationship was different for low-, medium-, and high-activity groups.

⭐

What I Found

☀️ Temperature mattered the most.

Warmer days were associated with higher average daily steps across all three activity groups.

🌧️ Rain was weaker than expected.

I expected rainy days to clearly reduce activity, but precipitation did not show a strong negative pattern.

☁️ Clouds may matter a little.

Very cloudy days were associated with slightly lower steps, but the relationship was not as clear as temperature.

🏁

Conclusion

Overall, this project suggests that weather is related to daily activity. Temperature was the clearest weather factor: warmer days were generally associated with more steps. However, this does not prove that weather directly caused people to walk more. The data shows association, not causation.

If I had more time, I would add sleep, study time, or screen time data to measure productivity more directly.

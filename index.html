Does Weather Change How Much We Move?

A data science story about temperature, clouds, rain, and daily steps.

Start the Story ↓
🌈

My Big Question

I wanted to know whether weather conditions are related to daily activity. Since productivity is difficult to measure directly, I used average daily steps as a simple proxy for productivity-related behavior.

This does not mean that steps perfectly measure productivity. Instead, steps give one measurable way to study daily routine, movement, and behavior across different weather conditions.

👟 Activity Data

Daily wearable activity records from 113 participants in Norway.

🌦️ Weather Data

Temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover data from Open-Meteo.

🧪 Method

I cleaned, grouped, aggregated, and merged the datasets by date.

❓

Research Questions

My project focuses on how weather is connected to physical activity. I used the following questions to guide the analysis:

  • Does warmer weather relate to higher average daily steps?
  • Do low-, medium-, and high-activity participants respond differently?
  • Are rainy days associated with lower activity?
  • Does cloud cover change daily step counts?
🧹

How I Prepared the Data

The two datasets did not match perfectly at first. The activity dataset was organized by participant and date, while the weather dataset included weather measurements over time. To make the datasets work together, I cleaned the date columns, summarized weather by day, and then merged the two datasets using matching dates.

New Technique: Regression with Interaction Terms

A normal regression model can show whether temperature and steps are related overall. However, my project also asks whether different activity groups respond differently to temperature.

To answer that, I used an interaction term between temperature and activity group. This allowed the model to estimate different temperature patterns for low-, medium-, and high-activity participants.

steps ~ temperature × activity group
⭐

What I Found

☀️ Temperature mattered the most.

Warmer days were associated with higher average daily steps across all three activity groups.

🌧️ Rain was weaker than expected.

I expected rainy days to clearly reduce activity, but precipitation did not show a strong negative pattern in this dataset.

☁️ Clouds may matter a little.

Cloudier days were associated with slightly lower steps, but this relationship was not as clear as the relationship between temperature and steps.

⚠️

Important Limitations

This project shows association, not causation. Even if warmer days are linked with more steps, this does not prove that temperature directly caused people to walk more.

Another limitation is that physical activity is only a proxy for productivity-related behavior. A person can be productive without walking a lot, and a person can walk a lot without being academically or professionally productive.

How I Built This Project

I combined a Norway wearable activity dataset with Open-Meteo weather data. The activity dataset included daily step counts and activity measures for 113 participants, while the weather dataset included temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover.

The main challenge was making the two datasets work together. The activity data was daily participant-level data, but the weather data had to be cleaned and summarized into daily weather values. After that, I grouped participants into low-, medium-, and high-activity groups and merged the datasets by date.

I used Python, pandas, Plotly, and statsmodels to clean the data, create interactive visualizations, and build a regression model with interaction terms.

What This Reveals

Overall, the data suggests that weather conditions are connected to daily physical activity. Temperature showed the clearest pattern: warmer days were generally linked with more steps. This pattern appeared across low-, medium-, and high-activity groups.

However, the results were not equally strong for every weather variable. Precipitation was weaker than expected, and cloud cover only showed a small relationship with activity. This suggests that temperature may be a more useful predictor of daily movement than rain or clouds in this dataset.

If I extended this project, I would add more direct productivity measures, such as study time, sleep, screen time, or assignment completion. That would make it possible to study productivity more directly instead of using daily steps as a proxy.

🏁

Conclusion

My main conclusion is that weather does seem related to daily activity, especially through temperature. Warmer days were generally associated with higher average daily steps, while rain and cloud cover showed weaker patterns.

The project also helped me understand how important data cleaning and merging are in real data science work. The final visualizations only made sense after the two datasets were carefully prepared and combined.
